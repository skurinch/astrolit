I am an astrophysics student from Caltech, and a U.S. Fulbright Scholar at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy. I love astrophysical simulations because they tell a story by allowing us to picture how the Universe and its galaxies came to be. Similarly, I always endeavor to create a narrative about my research that can reach a wide audience.

Like the famous nineteenth-century women astronomers, I am a “woman computer” myself, though in a unique sense: I want to use computation and astrophysics to simulate our Universe. Modern astrophysical simulations have made a breakthrough in this field: they can naturally realize observations of the Universe in the full cosmological context, with an unprecedented combination of numerical resolution and volume. Nevertheless, the data from these new simulations are vast, complex, and introduce more questions about the nature of our Universe.

Through my undergraduate studies at Caltech, I thus came to identify myself as an aspiring theoretical astrophysicist to meet the field at an exciting time of discovery. I have worked on numerous research projects in astrophysical simulations—from large-scale structure, to galaxies, to black holes—always connecting my findings to observational data from new telescope surveys. Since then, my love for the field has come far. I am now at the Max-Planck-Institut für Astronomie in Germany on the U.S. Fulbright Scholarship, exploring galaxy evolution with the state-of-the-art simulation of the Universe, IllustrisTNG, all the while immersing myself in international efforts for public engagement with science.

Astronomy tells a story, too, though one from the beginning of time: the story of our Universe, and our place in it.
