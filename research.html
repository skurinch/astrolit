AGN and Quenching in Local, Isolated Dwarf Galaxies

On the origin of star formation quenching in nearby, isolated dwarf galaxies in the IllustrisTNG Universe, in collaboration with Dr. Michaela Hirschmann at the Dark Cosmology Center in Copenhagen, Denmark.

Recent observations of local, isolated dwarf galaxies find hints for active galactic nuclei (AGN) activity in terms of their emission-line ratios, potentially suggesting that star formation in these galaxies was quenched due to feedback from the central AGN. Theoretically, however, numerical simulations have repeatedly shown that AGN feedback mostly regulates star formation in massive galaxies, whereas low-mass galaxies are predominantly affected by stellar feedback. We take advantage of the state-of-the-art cosmological simulation IllustrisTNG as well as synthetic emission-line models, with a particular focus on isolated dwarf galaxies in the local Universe to investigate possible origin of AGN-like emission-line ratios in isolated quenched dwarfs. In TNG, AGN-like emission-line signatures in quiescent dwarfs are indeed due to ionization from the central AGN, rather than other ionizing sources. However, this by no means implies that quiescent dwarfs are quenched because of AGN feedback in the simulation as environmental conditions also play a very dominant role in initiating the quenching process.

Causes of Quiescence in Early, Massive Galaxies

On the origin of star-formation quenching in massive galaxies at z≳3 in the cosmological simulations IllustrisTNG, in collaboration with Dr. Michaela Hirschmann at École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL) and Dr. Francesco Valentino at the Cosmic Dawn Center in Copenhagen, Denmark.

The goal is to understand what suppresses their star formation so early in cosmic time, and how other similar mass galaxies remain highly star-forming. As a first-order result, the IllustrisTNG simulations are able to produce massive, quiescent galaxies in this high-redshift regime. We find that active galactic nuclei (AGN) feedback is the primary cause of halting star formation in early, massive galaxies. Not only do the central, supermassive black holes (SMBHs) of the quenched galaxies have earlier seed times, but they also grow faster than in star-forming galaxies. As a result, the quenched galaxies are exposed to AGN feedback for longer, and experience the kinetic mode of the AGN feedback earlier than the star-forming galaxies. While mergers do not play a significant role in the quenching process, the quenched galaxies tend to reside in more massive halos and denser regions during their evolution. As this provides a greater initial amount of infalling gas to the galaxies, the large-scale environment can mildly affect the fate of the central SMBH growth and, via AGN feedback, contribute to star formation quenching.

Presence of Supermassive Black Holes in Jellyfish Galaxies

Jellyfish galaxies with the IllustrisTNG simulations – Supermassive black hole activity in ram-pressure stripped galaxies, in collaboration with Dr. Annalisa Pillepich at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy in Heidelberg, Germany.

Jellyfish galaxies are an extreme example of how galaxies can transform due to residing in dense cluster cosmological environments These satellite galaxies are strongly affected by ram-pressure stripping, leading to the formation of their gaseous tails. However, a significant number of jellyfish galaxies are also hosts of active galactic nuclei (AGN). Observations and simulations give apparently contradictory results as to the link between ram-pressure stripping and AGN feedback, necessitating larger samples and more controlled theoretical experiments. We use the IllustrisTNG cosmological gravitational + magnetohydrodynamical simulations to explore the presence of AGN in the origin of jellyfish galaxies from the “Zooniverse Cosmological Jellyfish” citizen-science project. Compared to the population of satellite galaxies, jellyfish have more luminous supermassive black holes, and thus higher AGN fractions. Ram-pressure acts to increase densities of the gas in the centers of jellyfish galaxies, decreasing their sound speeds and driving supermassive black hole accretion, further distinguishing them from other inspected satellites.

Spherical Collapse of Dark Matter Halos

Spherical Collapse of Fuzzy Dark Matter in 3-D Simulations, in collaboration with Dr. Andrew Benson at the Carnegie Observatories in Pasadena, California.

In modern cosmological simulations, dark matter builds the backbone for galaxy formation through the process of halo collapse. While the current dark matter paradigm, i.e. cold dark matter, is able to capture the large-scale structure of the Universe, it predicts more small-scale structure (in the form of halos) than is actually observed. This issue may be resolved with the theory of fuzzy dark matter, which is made of ultra-light particles whose wavelike properties manifest on sub-galactic scales. The mechanism that suppresses halo collapse in this model is called quantum pressure, and arises from the Uncertainty principle. However, its behavior has yet to be fully understood in present theoretical models due to resolution limits. Using 3-D numerical simulations of individual halos, we performed a comprehensive analysis of how quantum pressure affects the collapse of fuzzy dark matter halos. Then, we made use of a semi-analytic treatment to predict the halo mass function for this theory. We find that fuzzy dark matter is able to delay the collapsing process and lead to less compact halo cores.
